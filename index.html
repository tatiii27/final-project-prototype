Writeup

1. What have we done so far?

We have created the scrollytelling globe at the top that highlights key stories for each region when the user scrolls down. We have also created the explore globe at the bottom that allows users to select different metrics and decades to see how countries compare. Additionally, we have started working on the life path section that allows users to choose a region, year, and gender to gamify the experience. We have also workshopped possible interactive features and are aiming to fine-tune those desgins and possibly add more data and features to visualizations.

2. What will be the most challenging of your project to design and why?

The most challenging part of our project to design will be the interactive components. We want to engage users in a way that is both informative and interactive, which requires careful consideration of user experience and interface design. Ensuring that the interactive elements are intuitive and accessible to a wide audience will be a key challenge. We also have a plethora of ideas for interactivity, so narrowing down to the most effective ones will be important. We are trying to strike a balance between guided storytelling and user-driven exploration, which can be difficult to achieve.

3. What do we plan to do next?

We plan on adding different dimensions of data story telling to each globe by region. For example, in the scrollytelling globe, we want to highlight key stories for each region about gender differences in education, life expectancy, socioeconmic status and other domains. In the game-styled life path section, we want to refine the user experience and add more data points to make the experience more immersive. To do this, we might animate features of a person as they add data features to explore their path and customize the data experience as features are added.

Africa

In Sub-Saharan Africa, girls started far behind boys in secondary and tertiary school, with big gaps that narrowed but did not disappear by 2010–2020.

1960-1970

Very low access to secondary and university for both genders, with boys slightly ahead.

2010–2020

School access improved, but boys still keep an edge in both secondary and tertiary enrollment.

Asia

In South Asia, boys were far ahead in the 1960s. By 2010–2020, secondary gaps almost vanished, but women are only slightly behind in university.

1960-1970

Boys dominated secondary and especially tertiary enrollment.

2010–2020

Secondary is almost equal; university is close but still slightly favors men.

Middle East & North Africa

From low enrollment for everyone, the region moved toward high schooling with women now slightly ahead in university.

1960-1970

Secondary and tertiary education were limited, especially for girls.

2010–2020

Secondary is high for both; women slightly outnumber men in university.

Europe

Europe started with small gaps and ended with women clearly ahead in higher education.

1960-1970

Secondary schooling was already widespread with tiny gender gaps.

2010–2020

Both genders nearly universal in secondary; women pull ahead in university.

Latin America & the Caribbean

Latin America went from low access to near-universal schooling, with women now leading in higher education.

1960-1970

Only a minority reached secondary school; men had more access than women.

2010–2020

Secondary access is high, and women are well ahead in university.

Northern America

In North America, men dominated university access in the 1960s; by 2010–2020, the gap flipped and women are far ahead.

1960-1970

Limited data for secondary; university enrollment clearly favored men.

2010–2020

Secondary is nearly universal; university now strongly favors women.

Oceania

In Oceania, secondary schooling was already high; over time women surged ahead in university enrollment.

1960-1970

High secondary schooling for both, but men much more likely to attend university.

2010–2020

Secondary is very high; women become the clear majority in university.
